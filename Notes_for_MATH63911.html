$\S$ 1.1 Polynomials and Affine Space¶

     A field is a set $k$ with two binary operations, $+$ (addition) and $*$ (multiplication), with at least two elements $0$ and $1$ (the smallest field), which obey the axioms of addition and subtraction, multiplication and division, and the special axiom of distributive law. The common application of fields is found in linear algebra, where fields are used to form the vector space where scalar and matrix operation operate under. In essence a vector space $V$ is a subset (subspace) of a zero characteristic commutative field, where the elements of matrices found in the later. Details of this generalization could be explore further by the reader in the following reading material.
     Though to elabroate the point, an arbitrary field does not need to comply with all axioms of operations, in fact these ommission create special characteristics to an arbitrary field. The most commonly seen and used arbitrary field is the vector field found in linear algebra, this unique field forms the basis for vector and matrix operation, but does not comply with the commutative law under the axioms of multiplication and division. In other example of arbitrary fields other axiom of operation is be omitted to derive special characterized fields specific to address specific mathematical problems that require certain field characteristics.

Definition 1. A field consists of a set of k and two binary operations "+" and "." destined on k for which the following conditions are satisfied:
(i) $(a+b)+c=a+(b+c)$ and $(a*b)*c=a(b*c)$ for all $a$,$b$,$c\in k$ (associativity).
(ii) $a+b=b+a$ and $a*b=b*a$ for all $a,b,c \in k$ (commutativity).
(iii) $a*(b+c)=a*b+a*c$ for all $a,b,c \in k$ (distributivity).
(iv) There are $0$,$1 \in k$ such that $a+0=a*1=a$ for all $a \in k$ (identities).
(v) Given $a \in k$, there is $b \in k$ such that $a+b=0$ (additive inverses).
(vi) Given $a \in k$, $a\ne0$, there is $c \in k$ such that $a*c=1$ (multiplicative inverses).

Definition 1. A monomial in $x_1,...,x_n$ is a product of the form $$x_1^{\alpha_1}*x_2^{\alpha_2}*\cdots*x_n^{\alpha_n}$$ where all of exponents $\alpha_1$,...,$\alpha_n$ are nonnegative integers. The **total degree** of this monomuial is the sum $\alpha_1+\cdots\alpha_n$.

     In essence a monomial is a polynomial with one term, as stated in its name's prefix "mono-" which means one in latin, and suffix "-nomial" for term; simply translating as one term. A simplified notation for a monomial, is $|\alpha|=\alpha_1+\cdots+\alpha_n$ which is the total degree of a monomial.

Definition 2. A polynomial $f$ in $x_1$,...,$x_n$ with coefficients in a field $k$ is a finite linear combination (with coefficients in $k$) of monomials. We will write a polynomial $f$ in the form $$f=\sum_{\alpha}^{}a_\alpha x^{\alpha}, a_\alpha \in k, $$ where the sum is over a finite number of *n*-tuples $\alpha=(\alpha_1,\cdots,\alpha_n)$. The set of all polynomials in $x_1$,...,$x_n$ with coefficients in $k$ is denoted $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$.

     The set of all polynomials $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$ is not a field as the set does not form with the axiom of multiplicative inverse, and therefore cannot form a normal closed field. Though the set of all rational polynomials can form a closed field because it possess qoutient polynomials which obey the axiom of multiplicative inverses, which is not conversely true [2].
     Despite $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$ not being a field, it is instead a special type of commutative ring known as a polynomial ring.

Commutative Ring: A commutative ring is a set $k$ with two binary operations, $+$ (addition) and $*$ (multiplication), which satisfies the conditions (i) through (v) of defintion 1, but not the condition (iv) of multiplicative inverses, as none exists. All sets that are fields, are commutative rings, but the converse is not true as commutative rings are not fields.

     Though the set of all polynomials is not a field, it is instead a commative ring, it is better known as the polynomial ring. The polynomial ring is a set of all polynomials that satisfies conditions (i) through (v) of definition 1.

Proponent 5. Let $k$ be an infinite field and let $f \in k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$. The $f=0$ ($f$ being the zero polynomial) in $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$ if and only if $f$: $k^{n} \rightarrow k$ is zero function.
PROOF. One direction of the proof is obvious since the zero polynomial clearly gives the zero function. To prove the converse, we need to show that if $f(a_1,\cdots,a_n)=0$ for all $(a_1,\cdots,a_n) \in k^{n}$, then $f$ is the zero polynomial. We will use induction on the number of variables $n$.
     When $n=1$, it is well known that a nonzero polynomial in $k[x]$ of degree $m$ has at most $m$ distant roots (we will prove this fact in Corollary 3 of $\S$5). For our particular $f \in k[x]$, we are assuming $f(a)=0$ for all $a \in k$. Since $k$ is infinite, this means that $f$ has infinitely many roots, and hence, $f$ must be the zero polynomial.
     Now assume that the converse is true for $n-1$, and let $f \in k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$ be a polynomial that vanishes at all points of $k^{n}$. By collecting the various powers of $x_n$, we can write $f$ in the form $$f=\sum_{i=0}^{N}g_i(x_1,\cdots,x_{n-1})x_n^{i},$$ where $g_i \in k[x_1,\cdots,n_{n-1}]$. We will show that each $g_i$ is the zero polynomial in $n-1$ variables, which will force $f$ to be the zero polynomial in $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$.
     If we fix $(a_1,\cdots,a_{n-1}) \in k^{n-1}$, we get the polynomial $f(a_1,\cdots,a_{n-1},x_n) \in k[x_n]$. By our hypothesis on $f$, this vanishes for every $a_n \in k$. It follows from the case $n=1$ that $f(a_1,\cdots,a_n-1,x_n)$ is the zero polynomial in $k[x_n]$. Using the above formula for $f$, we see that the coefficients of $f(a_1,\cdots,a_n-1,x_n)$ are $g_i(a_1,\cdots,a_n-1)$, and thus, $g_i(a_1,\cdots,a_n-1)=0$ for all $i$. Since $(a_1,\cdots,a_n-1)$ was arbitrarily chsen in $k^{n-1}$, it follows that each $g_i \in k[x_1,\cdots,x_n-1]$ gives the zero function on $k^{n-1}$. Our inductive assumption then implies that each $g_i$ is the zero polynomial in $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n-1]$. This forces $f$ to be the zero polynomial in $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$ and completes the proof of the proposition. $\square$

Corollary 6: Let $k$ be an infinite field, and let $f$, $g \in k[x_1,...,x_n]$. Then $f=g$ in $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$ if and only if $f$:$k^{n} \rightarrow k$ and $g$:$k^{n} \rightarrow k$ are the same function.
PROOF. To prove the nontrivial direction, suppose that $f$, $g \in k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$ give the same function on $k^{n}$. By hypothesis, the polynomial $f-g$ vanishes at all points of $k^{n}$. Proposition 5 then implies that $f-g$ is the zero polynomial. This proves that $f=g$ in $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$. $\square$

$\S$ 1.2 Affine Varities¶

Definition 1. Let $k$ be a field, and let $f_1$,...,$f_s$ be a polynomials in $k[x_1,\cdots,x_n]$. Then we set $$ \textbf{V}(f_1,\cdots,f_s)=[{(a_1,\cdots,a_n) \in K^{k}|f_i(a_1,\cdots,a_n)=0 \text{ for all } 1 \leq i \leq s}].$$ We call $\textbf{V}(f_1,\cdots,f_s)$ the affine variety defined by $f_1$,...,$f_s$.

     In essence an affine variety $\text{V}(f_1,...,f_s) \subseteq k^{n}$ is the set of all solution of a system of polynomail equations, such as,

$$ \begin{alignat*}{7} f_1(x_1,...&,x_n) = 0, \\ \vdots & & \\ f_s(x_1,...&,x_n) = 0. \\ \end{alignat*} $$

Algebraic variety:

Affine Variety:

For an algebraically closed field $k$ and a natural number $n$, let $A^{n}$ be affine n-space over $k$. The polynomials $f$ in the (polynomial) ring $k[x_1,...,x_n]$ can be viewed as $k$-valued function on $A^{n}$ by evaluating $f$ at the points in $A^{n}$, i.e. by choosing values in $k$ for each $x_i$. For each set $S$ of polynomials in $k[x_1,...,x_n]$, define the zero-locus $Z(S)$ to be the set of points in $A^{n}$ on which the function in $S$ simultaneously vanish, that is to say

$$Z(S)-\{x \in A^{n} | f(x)=0 \text{ for all } f \in S\}$$

A subset $V$ of $A^{n}$ is called an affine algebraic set if $V=Z(S)$ for some $S$. A nonempty affine algebraic set $V$ is called irreducible if it cannot be written as the union of two proper algebraic subsets. An irreducible affine algebraic set is also called an affine variety.

(Wikipedia): Algebraic variety: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety

Affine variety:

     An affine variety V is an algebraic variety contained in affine space. For example, $$(x,y,z):x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2}=0)$$ is the cone, and $$[{(x,y,z):x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2}=0,ax+by+cz=0}]$$ is a conic section, which is a subvariety of the cone. The cone can be written $\text{V}(x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2})$ to indicate that it is the variety corresponding to $x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2}=0$. Naturally, many other polynomials vanish on $\text{V}(x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2})$, in fact all polynomials in $I(C)={x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2}}$. The set $I(C)$ is an ideal in the polynomial ring $\C[x,y,z]$. Note also, that the ideal of polynomials vanishing on the conic section is the ideal generated by $x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2}$ and $ax+by+cz$.
     A morphism between two affine varieties is given by polynomial coordinate functions. For example, the map $\phi(x,y,z)=(x^{2},y^{2},z^{2})$ is a morphism from $X=\text{V}(x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2})$ to $Y=\text{V}(x+y+z)$. Two affine varieties are isomorphic if there is a morphism which has an inverse morphism. For example, the affine variety $\text{V}(x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2})$ is isomorphic to the cone $\text{V}(x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2})$ via the coordinate change $\phi(x,y,z)=(x,y,iz)$.
     Many polynomials $f$ may be factored, for instance $f=x^{2}+y^{2}=(x+iy)(x-iy)$, and then $\text{V}(f)=\text{V}(x+iy)$ union $\text{V}(x-iy)$. Consequently, only irreducible polynomials, and more generally only prime ideals $p$ are used in the definition of a variety. An affine variety $\text{V}$ is the set of common zeros of a collection of polynomials $p_1,\cdots,p_k$, i.e., $$\text{V}=[x=(x_1,\cdots,x_n):p_1(x)=\cdots=p_k(x)=0]$$ as long as the ideal $I=<p_1,...,p_k>$ is a prime ideal. More classically, an affine variety is defined by any set of polynomials, i.e., what is now called an algebraic set. Most points in $\text{V}$ will have dimension $n-k$, but $\text{V}$ may have singular points like the origin in the cone.
     When $\text{V}$ is one-dimensional generically (at almost all points), which typically occurs when $k=n-1$, then $\text{V}$ is called a curve.
     When $\text{V}$ is two-dimensional, it is called a surface. In the case of CW-complex affine space, a curve is a Riemann surface, possibly with some singularities." (Wolfram.MathWorld)

Affine Transforms {Maps):

Let $(X,V,k)$ be an affine space of dimension at least two, with $X$ the point set and $V$ be the associated vector space over the field $k$. A semiaffine transformation $f$ of $X$ is a bijection of $X$ onto itself satisfying.

  1. If $S$ is a d-dimensional affine subspace of $X$, $f(S)$ is also a d-dimensional affine subspace of $X$.
  2. If $S$ and $T$ are parallel affine subspaces of $X$, then $f(S)$||$f(T)$. These two conditions express what is precisely meant by the expression that "$f$ preserves parallelism".
    These conditions are not independent as the second follows from the first. Furthermore, if the field $k$ has at least three elements, the first condition can be simplified to: $f$ is a collineation, that is, it maps lines to lines.
    If the dimension of the affine space $(X,V,k)$ is at least two, then an affine transformation is a semiaffine transformation $f$ that satisfies the condition: If $x \ne >y$ and $p \ne q$ are points of $X$ such that the line segments $\overline{xy}$ and >$\overline{pq}$ are parallel, then $$\frac{\| \overline{pq} \|}{\| \overline{xy} \|}=\frac{\| \overline{f(p)f(q)} \|}{\| \overline{f(x)f(y)} \|}."$$

(Wikipedia): Affube transformation: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation

Affine Line:

If the dimension of the affine space is one, that is, the space is an affine line, then any permutation of $X$ would automatically satisfy the conditions to be a semiaffine transform. So, an affine transformation of an affine line is defined as any permutation $f$ of the points of $X$ such that if $X \ne y$ and $p \ne q$ are points of $X$, then $$\frac{\| \overline{pq} \|}{\| \overline{xy} \|}=\frac{\| \overline{f(p)f(q)} \|}{\| \overline{f(x)f(y)} \|}."$$

$\S$ 1.4 Ideals¶

     Ideals are the means for computating with affine varieties.

Definition 1. A subset $I \subseteq k[x_1,...,x_n]$ is an ideal if it satisfies: (i) $0 \text{zero polynomial} \in I$
(ii) If (polynomials) $f$, $g \in I$, then $f+g \in I$.
(iii) If a polynomial $f \in I$ and (any polynomial) $h \in k[x_1,...,x_n]$, (which is part of an commutative ring $k[x_1,...,x_n]$), then $hf \in I$.

Ideals play an important roles in constructing computational Algebriac Geometric algorithms, as it is the language for affine varieties and affine spaces.      An natural first ideal to present the reader is the ideal that is generated by a finite number of polynomials.

Definition 2. Let $f_1,...,f_s$ be polynomials in $k[x_1,...,x_n]$. Then we set $$\langle f_1,...,f_s \rangle = \Big\{ \sum_{i=1}^{s} h_if_i | h_1,...,h_s \in k[x_1,...,x_n] \Big\}$$      The crucial fact is that $\langle f_1,...,f_s \rangle$ is an ideal (Cox et al., 2015).

Lemma 3. If $f_1,...,,f_s \in k[x_1,...,x_n]$ (polynomial ring), then $\langle f_1,...,f_s \rangle$ is an ideal of $k[x_1,...,x_n]$ (polynomial ring). We will call $\langle f_1,...,f_s \rangle$ the ideal generated by $f_1,...,f_s$.
PROOF. First, $0 \in \langle f_1,...,f_s \rangle$ since $0=\sum_{i=1}^{s} 0*f_i$. Next, suppose that $f=\sum_{i=1}^{s} p_if_i$ and $g=\sum_{i=1}^{s} q_if_i$, and let $h \in k[x_1,...,x_n]$. Then the equations $$f+g=\sum_{i=1}^{s} (p_i+q_i)f_i,$$ $$hf=\sum_{i=1}^{s} (hp_i)f_i$$ complete the proof that $\langle f_1,...,f_s \rangle$ is an ideal. $\square$

     In essence the ideal is generated by a linear combination of a set of polynomials $f_1,...,f_s$; where instead of multiplying a scalar value across the set of polynomials, a set of polynomials $h_1,...,h_s$ is multiplied with $f_1,...,f_s$ to form the ideal $\langle f_1,...,f_s \rangle$ through this linear combination, $h_1f_1+h_2f_2+\cdots+h_sf_s$.      In the text, the author reference to the results of the operation of adding, subtracting, multiplying and scaling polynomials is "polynomial consequence".

$\S$ 2 Orderings on Monomials in $k[x_1,...,x_n]$¶

Definition 1. A monomial ordering $>$ on $k[x_1,...,x_n]$ is a relation $>$ on $\Z_{\geq0}^{n}$, satisfying:
1. $>$ is a total (or linear) ordering on $\Z_{\geq0}^{n}$.
2. If $\alpha > \beta$ and $\gamma \in \Z_{\geq0}^{n}$, then $\alpha+\gamma > \beta+\gamma$.
3. $>$ is a well-ordering on $\Z_{\geq0}^{n}$. This means that every nonempty subset of $\Z_{\geq0}^{n}$ has a smallest element under $>$. In other words, if $A \subseteq \Z_{\geq0}^{n}$ is nonempty, then there is $\alpha \in A$ such that $\beta > \alpha$ for every $\beta \ne \alpha$ in $A$.

Lemma 2. An order relation $>$ on $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$ is a well-ordering if and only if every strictly decreasing sequence in $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$ $$\alpha(1) > \alpha(2) > \alpha(3) > \cdots$$ eventually terminates.
PROOF. We will prove this contrapositive form: $>$ is not a well-ordering if and only if there ais an infinite strictly decreasing sequence in $>$ on $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$.
     If $>$ is not a well-ordering, then some nonempty subset $S \subseteq \Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$ has no least element. Now pick $\alpha(1) \in S$. Since $\alpha(1)$ is not the least element, we can find $\alpha(1) > \alpha(2)$ in $S$. Then $\alpha(2)$ is also not the least element, so there is $\alpha(2) > \alpha(3)$ in $S$. Continuing this way, we get an infinite strictly decreasing sequence $$\alpha(1) > \alpha(2) > \alpha(3) > \cdots.$$ Conversely, given such an infinite sequence, then $\{\alpha(1), \alpha(2) \alpha(3),...\}$ is a non-empty subset of $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$ with no least element, and thus, $>$ (ordering) is not a well-ordering. $\square$

     For an sequence to possess an smallest element, it must terminate, otherwise the sequence could possess exceedingly smaller element somewhere in its infinite sequence. And here is the importance of Lemma 2, as any algorithm must terminate, the sequence of terms that is feed into the algorithm must be finite.

Definition 3 (Lexicographic Order). Let $\alpha = (\alpha_1,...,\alpha_n)$ and $\beta = (\beta_1,...,\beta_n)$ be in $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$. We say say $\alpha >_{\text{lex}} \beta$ if the lestmost nonzero entry of the vector difference $\alpha - \beta \in \Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$ is positive. We will write $x^{\alpha} >_{\text{lex}} x^{\beta}$ if $\alpha >_{\text{lex}} \beta$.

The variables $x_1,...,x_n$ are ordered in the usual way (means) by the lex ordering: $$(1,0,...,0) >_{\text{Lex}} (0,1,0,...,0) >_{\text{Lex}} \cdots >_{\text{Lex}} (0,0,...,0,1).$$ so $x_1 >_{\text{Lex}} x_2 >_{\text{Lex}} \cdots >_{\text{Lex}} x_n$.

Proposition 4. The lex ordering on $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$ is a monomial ordering.
PROOF. (i) That $>_{\text{Lex}}$ is a total ordering follow directly from the definition and the fact that the usual numerical order on $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$ is a total ordering.
     (ii) If $\alpha >_{\text{Lex}} \beta$, then we have that the leftmost nonzero entry in $\alpha-\beta$, say $\alpha_i - \beta_i$, is positive. But $x^{\alpha}*x^{\gamma}=x^{\alpha + \gamma}$ and $x^{\beta}*x^{\gamma}=x^{\beta + \gamma}$. Then in $(\alpha + \gamma)$ - $(\beta - \gamma)=\alpha - \beta$, the leftmost nonzero entry is again (non-negataive/positive) $\alpha_i - \beta_i > 0$.
     (iii) Suppose that $>_{\text{Lex}}$ were not a well-ordering. Then by Lemma 2, there would be an infinitely strictly descending sequence $$\alpha(1) >_{\text{Lex}} \alpha(2) >_{\text{Lex}} \alpha(3) >_{\text{Lex}} \cdots$$ of elements of $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$. We will show that this leads to a contradiction.
     Consider the first entries of the vectors $\alpha(i) \in \Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$. By the definition of the lex order, these first entries form a nonincreasing sequence of nonnegative itegers. Since $\Z_{\geq 0}^{n}$ is well-ordered, first entries of the $\alpha(i)$ must "stablize" eventually. In other words, there exists an $l$ such that all the first entries of the $\alpha(i)$ with $i \geq l$ are equal.
     Beginning at $\alpha(l)$, the second and subsequent entries come into play in determining the lex order. The second entries of $\alpha(l), \alpha(l+1),...$ form a nonincreasing sequence. By the same reasoning as bvefore, the second entries "stablize" eventually as well. Continuing in the same way, we see that for some $m$, the $\alpha(m),\alpha(,+1),...$ all are equal. This contradicts the fact that $\alpha(m) >_{\text{Lex}} \alpha(m+1)$. $\square$

     Simply proposition 4: states that the various lex ordering arrangements are able arrange terms of a polynomial in terms of a monomial ordering.
